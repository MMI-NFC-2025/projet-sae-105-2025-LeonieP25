The place of women in scientific literature

For centuries, women were systematically excluded from scientific institutions and academic recognition. Their contributions, often attributed to male colleagues, were erased from the official history of science.

The underrepresentation of women in current scientific literature perpetuates these imbalances. Textbooks and popular accounts continue to make women’s contributions invisible, reinforcing stereotypes and discouraging girls from pursuing science.

The Matilda Effect

Named after Matilda Joslyn Gage, this phenomenon describes the systematic denial of women’s scientific contributions, often credited to their male colleagues.

Forgotten figures and representation issues

Women who succeeded in science had to be twice as good as their male colleagues to get half the recognition.

Timeline of forgotten women scientists

  • Mary Whiton Calkins 1863–1930 American psychologist, pioneer in the study of memory and the self-concept, denied a doctorate by Harvard.
  • Lise Meitner 1878–1968 Austrian physicist, co-discoverer of nuclear fission, ignored by the Nobel Prize awarded to Otto Hahn.
  • Chien-Shiung Wu 1912–1997 Chinese-American physicist, her experiments on parity earned a Nobel for her male colleagues.
  • Rosalind Franklin 1920–1958 British biophysicist, her images of DNA enabled the discovery of its structure without her being recognized.
  • Esther Lederberg 1922–2006 American microbiologist, pioneer of bacterial genetics, overshadowed by her Nobel-winning husband.
  • Jocelyn Bell Burnell 1943 British astrophysicist, discoverer of the first pulsar, not included in the Nobel awarded to her supervisor.

Excerpts and references to literary works

“The Forgotten Women of Science”

Margaret Rossiter
Editions Ellipses, 2018

Danielle Fauque documents the careers of many French women scientists.

More references to literary works:

  • “Inferior” — Angela Saini, Beacon Press, 2017. Explores how science has historically minimized or misinterpreted the role of women, and how these biases persist today.
  • “Hidden Figures” — Margot Lee Shetterly, HarperCollins, 2016. Reveals the little-known story of African-American mathematicians who contributed to NASA’s success.
  • “Lab Girl” — Hope Jahren, Knopf, 2016. Explores the experience of a woman scientist today.
  • “The Discoverers” — Yaël Nazé, Vuibert, 2014. Traces the journey of twenty women who revolutionized science.
  • “The Madame Curie Complex” — Julie Des Jardins, Feminist Press, 2010. Analyzes how women scientists have had to navigate between recognition and conformity.
